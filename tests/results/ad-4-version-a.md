### Top 3 Recommendations

1. **Research (9/25):** Replace the catch-all audience qualifier "if you've ever looked at your marketing spend and thought 'there has to be a better way'" with a named ICP — e.g., "DTC e-commerce brands spending $10K-$50K/month on paid social." Add one line of audience-specific pain language early in the copy (after the spreadsheet paragraph) that names their world: specific ad platforms they're running, typical order values, or category (supplements, apparel, skincare). Right now the copy reads as "anyone with a marketing budget," which dilutes relevance and lowers click-through from the people most likely to convert.
2. **Mechanism (12/25):** Name the mechanism and give it a specific explanation chain. "It studies your top-performing campaigns, identifies the patterns that actually drive conversions, and reverse-engineers them into new creative that keeps winning" is a feature description, not a mechanism. Rewrite this paragraph to introduce a named process (e.g., "AdStack's Pattern Extraction Engine analyzes your last 90 days of conversion data, isolates the 3-5 creative variables most correlated with purchase — headline structure, image composition, CTA placement — and recombines them into new variations weighted toward your highest-ROAS patterns"). This transforms a vague "it learns and improves" into a believable, differentiated explanation of WHY it works.
3. **Brief (18/25):** Add one line of competitive differentiation against other AI ad tools — not just human agencies. The entire angle positions AdStack vs. agencies, but the real purchase decision for a solution-aware buyer is "why this AI tool and not Jasper, Copy.ai, or another AI ad generator?" After the line "That's when I found AdStack," insert a brief contrast: "I'd already tried three AI copywriting tools — they all spit out the same bland, template-driven ads. AdStack was different because..." This closes the positioning gap and preempts the obvious objection.
