## RMBC Copy Audit

**Copy type:** Ad (direct response email/social ad — subject line, narrative body, CTA, P.S.)
**Overall score:** 63/100 — Grade: C
**Severity summary:** 1 Rewrite | 3 Major Fix | 3 Minor Fix

### Dimension Scores

| Dimension | Score | Severity | Key Finding |

### Findings

**Rewrite:**
- **R (Research):** The copy never identifies its target audience. "If you've ever looked at your marketing spend and thought 'there has to be a better way'" could address anyone from a $5K/mo e-commerce store to a $500K/mo DTC brand. The narrator mentions "CAC," "ROAS," and paying $4,700/mo for an agency — signaling sophistication — yet the copy never mirrors a specific audience's language, industry, or situation. A DTC brand owner scaling on Meta has fundamentally different pain points than a B2B SaaS founder running Google Ads. Without ICP specificity, the copy cannot match audience awareness level with confidence. -> Define and name the ICP explicitly in the first 3 lines. Example: "If you're a DTC brand doing $1M+ and spending $30K-$100K/month on paid media..." This anchors every claim that follows and lets the reader self-select.

**Major Fix:**
- **M (Mechanism):** "It studies your top-performing campaigns, identifies the patterns that actually drive conversions, and reverse-engineers them into new creative that keeps winning" describes what any AI ad platform would claim. There is no named system, no proprietary process, no specific explanation of WHY this particular approach works when others don't. -> Name the mechanism and make it specific. Example: "AdStack's PatternMatch Engine analyzes the 23 micro-signals in your winning ads — headline structure, image composition, offer framing — and recombines them into new variations that preserve what's working." A named mechanism is memorable, differentiating, and gives the reader a mental model to anchor belief.
- **R (Research):** Pain points are told through the narrator's personal story ("my agency... my CAC... my spreadsheet") rather than demonstrated through audience-mirrored language. The reader must map the narrator's experience to their own situation. There is no "you" framing of pain until the vague bridge line near the end. -> Rewrite the bridge section to mirror the reader's pain directly. After the narrator's story, add 2-3 lines that call out the reader's specific version: "Maybe your agency sends you a 40-page report every month that somehow never mentions ROAS. Maybe you've watched your CAC climb from $18 to $54 while they celebrate 'impressions.'" This proves you've done the research on their world.
- **C (Copy):** All proof comes from a single anonymous narrator: "$23,400 in revenue on $2,100 spend," "47 ad variations," "12 outperformed." These numbers are strong individually, but single-source proof is thin. No testimonials, no named businesses, no aggregate data, no third-party validation. -> Stack at least two additional proof types: a named testimonial ("Sarah M., DTC skincare brand — 'AdStack replaced our $6K/mo agency in week one'"), plus aggregate data ("2,400+ brands have switched from agency to AdStack, averaging 4.2x ROAS in their first 60 days"). Three proof types is the minimum for high-ticket conversion.

**Minor Fix:**
- **C (Copy):** CTA "Start your 7-day free trial at adstack.io. Cancel anytime." is clear and single but lacks urgency. No deadline, no scarcity, no consequence for delay. -> Add a time-bound element: "Start your 7-day free trial before March 15 — they're capping new accounts at 500 this month" or "Start your free trial today. The brands signing up this week get 47 AI-generated ad variations in their first 24 hours."
- **B (Brief):** The agency-replacement angle is strong but limits addressable market. Many potential buyers have never used an agency — they're doing ads themselves and drowning. The entire narrative requires agency experience to resonate. -> Add a secondary entry point in the P.S. or bridge: "Never had an agency? Even better — you get agency-level creative without the $4,700/month learning curve."
- **M (Mechanism):** The mechanism has no name. "Reverse-engineers" is a verb, not a system. Named mechanisms ("The Dual Readership Path," "The 4U Framework") are stickier and more persuasive. -> Name it. Even "AdStack's Reverse-Engineering Engine" is better than nothing, but a distinctive name like "Creative DNA Mapping" would be stronger.

### Top 3 Recommendations (Priority Order)

1. **Rewrite — Research:** Define the ICP in the opening lines. Replace "I fired my marketing agency" with a version that names the audience: "If you run a DTC brand spending $30K+/month on ads, I need to tell you what happened when I fired my agency." Every downstream claim (CAC, ROAS, agency fees) gains credibility when the reader knows this person is them.
2. **Major Fix — Mechanism:** Name and specify the mechanism. "Reverse-engineers your winners" is a process summary, not a mechanism. Create a named system with a specific explanation chain: what it analyzes, how many signals it reads, why that produces winners. This is the single biggest differentiator gap — without it, AdStack sounds like every other AI ad tool.
3. **Major Fix — Copy:** Stack proof beyond a single narrator. Add a named testimonial and aggregate performance data. The copy's specificity (11.1x ROAS, $23,400 revenue) is excellent but needs corroboration. One story is an anecdote; three proof types is evidence.
