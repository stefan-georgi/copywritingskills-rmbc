## RMBC Copy Audit

**Copy type:** SaaS ad (short-form paid social/landing page ad)
**Overall score:** 41/100 — Grade: D
**Severity summary:** 2 Rewrite | 3 Major Fix | 3 Minor Fix

> **Escalation gate triggered:** 2 Rewrite-level findings detected. Recommend full rewrite addressing Research and Mechanism before investing in minor fixes. Polishing copy that targets everyone with no mechanism is optimizing a broken foundation.

### Dimension Scores

| Dimension | Score | Severity | Key Finding |
|-----------|-------|----------|-------------|
| R (Research) | 9/25 | Rewrite | ICP is "teams" — no segment, no specificity, no audience language |
| M (Mechanism) | 5/25 | Rewrite | No mechanism exists — "AI-driven features" is a buzzword, not an explanation of why this works |
| B (Brief) | 13/25 | Major Fix | Offer terms are clear but angle is generic "save time" with no differentiation or emotional driver |

### Findings

**Rewrite:**
- **M (Mechanism):** No mechanism present. "Our powerful AI-driven features streamline your workflow" is a claim, not an explanation. There is no answer to WHY TaskFlow Pro works better than Asana, Monday, or ClickUp. The feature list ("Smart task automation," "Advanced analytics dashboard") describes WHAT without ever explaining the underlying system or process that produces results. -> Name and develop a specific mechanism: what does the AI actually do, what pattern does it detect, what bottleneck does it eliminate? E.g., "TaskFlow's Dependency Engine maps your team's actual work patterns and auto-sequences tasks around your real blockers — not a template someone at Asana built in 2019."
- **R (Research):** The ICP is everyone. "Whether you're a startup or an enterprise" explicitly targets the entire market. Pain point "wasting time on manual processes" applies to literally any knowledge worker. No industry, no role, no budget range, no specific frustration. -> Pick one beachhead segment. E.g., "Series A SaaS teams burning 20+ engineer-hours/week on status meetings" gives you an ICP, a pain point with a number, and language that signals you understand their world.

**Major Fix:**
- **R (Research):** Pain points are assumed, not demonstrated. "Stop wasting time on manual processes" presumes the audience self-identifies with this problem, but the language is too generic to trigger recognition. No specific scenario — what manual process? Updating spreadsheets? Copy-pasting between tools? Chasing Slack threads for status updates? -> Replace with a concrete scenario the target segment would recognize instantly. Specificity is the mechanism of recognition.
- **B (Brief):** Zero competitive differentiation. Every project management tool claims AI, integrations, analytics, and security. "Seamless integrations with 200+ apps" is table stakes in 2026. Nothing in this copy answers "why not Monday/Asana/ClickUp?" -> Identify one genuine differentiator and make it the strategic centerpiece. If none exists at the product level, differentiate on audience ("built for X teams") or mechanism ("the only PM tool that does Y").
- **C (Copy):** Proof is thin and partially unsubstantiated. "Thousands of teams trust TaskFlow Pro" is an unverifiable vanity claim. "TaskFlow Pro changed the way we work" (Sarah T.) is a generic endorsement with no specifics. The Mike R. testimonial ("saved 15 hours per week") is the single strongest line in the entire ad — and it's buried near the bottom. -> Lead with the Mike R. data point or something equally specific. Replace "thousands of teams" with an exact number or a named company. Cut the Sarah T. testimonial or add specifics to it.

**Minor Fix:**
- **C (Copy):** Hook is generic. "Stop wasting time on manual processes" could open an ad for any of 500 SaaS tools. It doesn't stop the scroll because it doesn't surprise, challenge, or provoke. -> Test a hook built on a specific, counterintuitive claim: "Your team wastes 23% of sprint capacity on work-about-work. Here's the math." The specificity earns the next line.
- **C (Copy):** Vague adjective overload. "Powerful," "advanced," "seamless," "flexible," "enterprise-grade" — these are filler words that communicate nothing. -> Replace each with a concrete capability. "Advanced analytics" -> "See which tasks bottleneck your pipeline every sprint." "Enterprise-grade security" -> "SOC 2 Type II certified, SSO included."
- **B (Brief):** No emotional driver. The copy is entirely rational (features, pricing, trial). No stakes, no consequences of inaction, no aspiration beyond "efficiency." -> Add a line that connects to what the audience actually cares about: shipping faster than competitors, not losing their best engineers to burnout, or being the team that actually hits deadlines.

### Top 3 Recommendations (Priority Order)

1. **Rewrite — Mechanism:** Build a named mechanism that explains WHY TaskFlow Pro produces results. "AI-driven features" is not a mechanism. Describe the specific system, process, or approach — what it detects, what it automates, and why that particular automation matters more than what competitors offer. Without this, the ad is a feature list competing on price.
2. **Rewrite — Research:** Narrow the ICP from "teams" to a single segment with a named role, a quantified pain point, and language pulled from how that audience actually describes their frustration. "Startup or enterprise" is not targeting — it's surrender. Pick one, win them, then expand.
3. **Major Fix — Copy:** Restructure proof hierarchy. Move the "15 hours per week" data point to the hook or first proof section. Replace "thousands of teams" with a verifiable number or named customer. Cut or rewrite the Sarah T. testimonial to include a specific outcome.
